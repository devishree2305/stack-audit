# ECONOMICS.md

## Overview

Stack Audit is fundamentally a lead-generation and procurement-intelligence product rather than a standalone SaaS dashboard. The economic goal is not simply charging for audits. The goal is identifying companies with fragmented or oversized AI spend and converting them into higher-value procurement or credit customers for Credex.

The most important metric is therefore:

* qualified high-spend teams discovered per month

rather than raw traffic volume.

---

# 1. What Is a Converted Lead Worth to Credex?

I assume the highest-value downstream action is:

* helping startups purchase cloud/API credits
* procurement consolidation
* AI vendor financing or spend optimization services

A reasonable early-stage assumption:

* average startup AI spend discovered through Stack Audit:
  ~$2,000/month
* annualized AI spend:
  ~$24,000/year

If Credex captures:

* 5–15% margin/economic value through procurement, financing, referral agreements, or negotiated vendor partnerships

then one converted customer could realistically be worth:

* low case: ~$1,200/year
* medium case: ~$2,400/year
* high case: ~$4,000+/year

For modeling purposes, I would use:

* approximately $2,000 annual value per converted customer

That is high enough to justify relatively expensive acquisition channels if conversion quality is strong.

---

# 2. Estimated CAC by Channel

The advantage of Stack Audit is that the target user already publicly discusses AI tooling decisions online. That creates relatively low-cost distribution opportunities.

## Founder communities / Reddit / Indie Hackers

Acquisition method:

* manual posting
* discussions
* public audit teardown examples
* founder conversations

Estimated CAC:

* $0–$20

Mostly founder time rather than direct spend.

---

## Cold outbound using public AI-tool adoption signals

Example:

* companies publicly discussing Cursor, Claude, Copilot, or OpenAI usage

Estimated CAC:

* ~$40–$80

Main costs:

* founder/operator time
* outbound tooling
* manual personalization

But lead quality would likely be high because outreach is contextual.

---

## Viral report sharing

The public shareable report flow is potentially the lowest CAC channel.

Example:

* “Stack Audit found $9k/year in wasted AI spend”

If users organically share reports:

* CAC could approach near-zero for secondary users.

Estimated CAC:

* <$10

assuming strong share conversion.

# 3. What Conversion Rates Make This Profitable?

Using rough assumptions:

### Funnel assumptions

1. 1,000 visitors

2. 25% complete an audit
   = 250 audits

3. 20% submit lead information
   = 50 leads

4. 25% book a Credex consultation
   = 12–13 consultations

5. 30% convert into procurement/credit customers
   = 3–4 customers

If each converted customer is worth:

* ~$2,000/year

Then:

* 4 customers × $2,000
* = ~$8,000 annualized value

If acquisition cost for those 1,000 visitors remains:

* under ~$1,500–$2,000 total

then the economics become attractive fairly quickly.

The key insight is that Stack Audit does not need massive conversion rates if the downstream procurement value is meaningful.

---

# 4. What Would Need To Be True To Reach $1M ARR in 18 Months?

Using the earlier assumption:

* average annual value per converted customer:
  ~$2,000/year

To reach:

* $1,000,000 ARR

the system would need approximately:

* 500 active converted customers

within 18 months.

That roughly implies:

* ~28 new paying customers/month on average

Using the earlier funnel:

If:

* 30% of consultations convert
* 25% of leads book consultations
* 20% of audits become leads

then the platform would need approximately:

* ~1,900 completed audits/month

to sustain that conversion volume.

That is ambitious but not unrealistic if:

* public sharing loops work
* AI tooling spend continues increasing rapidly
* procurement consolidation becomes a larger operational problem for startups

The product becomes especially interesting if it evolves from:

* “AI savings calculator”

into:

* “AI procurement operating system.”

---

# 5. Key Economic Insight

The economics work only if Stack Audit acts as:

* a high-intent procurement signal generator

rather than just a generic AI productivity tool.

The strongest leads are not:

* hobby users
* solo consumers

The strongest leads are:

* growing engineering teams
* startups with overlapping AI tooling
* companies already spending thousands monthly on AI products

That is why the recommendation engine intentionally prioritizes:

* trustworthiness
* explainability
* finance-style reasoning

over exaggerated optimization claims.

If users trust the recommendations, they are far more likely to escalate procurement discussions through Credex.
