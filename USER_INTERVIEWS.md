## Interview 1: Selvakumar

**Role:** Works in a private company, previously built a small app project called “GameChanger”
**Context:** Casual AI-tool user, mostly uses ChatGPT occasionally for invitation designs, writing help, and small personal tasks

I showed the Stack Audit landing page and sample audit report to my mama because he has some experience building small software projects and usually notices UI/design direction quickly. Even though he is not the exact procurement target user, I wanted feedback on whether the product visually felt trustworthy or too “generic AI app.”

His first reaction was mostly about the visual style rather than the pricing logic itself. He said, “Right now every AI website looks the same. If this is about money and audits, it should feel more serious.” He also said, “Black and green would look stronger — more like finance or analytics software.” Another thing he mentioned was, “The report section feels useful, but the first thing people judge is whether the website itself looks trustworthy.”

He was less interested in the recommendation engine itself because he only uses ChatGPT casually, mostly for invitation wording and occasional writing tasks. One interesting comment was, “Normal people will not understand AI pricing plans properly unless the UI explains them very clearly.” That made me realize the language on the landing page needed to stay simple and less technical.

The most surprising thing he said was that the product looked “too startup-colorful” initially and should lean more into a darker professional aesthetic if it wants users to trust financial recommendations.

**Most surprising thing they said:** “People trust financial tools more when they look stable and minimal, not flashy.”

**What changed afterward:** I refined the UI direction to feel more finance-oriented and premium, leaned further into darker visual styling, reduced overly playful color usage, and focused more on visual trustworthiness in the report pages.

## Interview 2: Jasper

**Role:** Student
**Context:** College connection, regularly uses Gemini and ChatGPT for assignments, writing tasks, presentations, and general academic work

I showed the Stack Audit landing page and sample audit report to my friend Jasper because she actively uses multiple AI tools and usually notices usability issues quickly. Even though she is not managing company-wide AI procurement, I wanted feedback on whether the interface felt understandable and modern to someone already familiar with AI products.

Her first reaction was positive about the overall idea. She said, “It’s actually cool seeing all AI tools in one place instead of opening different websites all the time.” But she also immediately pointed out that she was unsure whether the product was meant only for companies or also for individuals. She said, “I only pay for one or two AI tools, so I don’t know if I’m the exact target user.”

A large part of her feedback was around usability and appearance. She said, “If people are spending time looking at reports and dashboards, there should definitely be both dark mode and light mode.” She also mentioned, “Some dashboards look nice in dark mode but become hard to read after some time.” Another comment she made was, “The report looks clean, but I would want slightly more explanation before trusting the savings suggestions.”

There was an interesting contradiction in her feedback. She initially said she prefers minimal pages, but later kept asking for more explanation around how the recommendations were calculated. That helped me realize the interface itself should stay visually minimal while the audit reasoning should remain detailed and transparent.

She also brought up privacy concerns around public sharing. One thing she said was, “If company names or emails are visible, nobody will share this publicly.”

**Most surprising thing they said:** She treated dark mode/light mode support as a trust and usability feature rather than just visual customization.

**What changed afterward:** I implemented proper dark/light theme support, improved readability across report sections, and made the public-share privacy handling more explicit so users would feel safer sharing audit results publicly.

## Interview 3: Aliza

**Role:** Final-year Computer Engineering student
**Context:** Familiar with AI tools, SaaS products, dashboards, and technical report-style interfaces through academic and project work

I showed the sample Stack Audit report page to my friend Aliza because she regularly works with technical dashboards and AI tools for projects and assignments. I specifically wanted feedback on whether the audit report felt understandable and trustworthy to someone who is technical but not deeply involved in procurement or finance workflows.

Her first reaction was focused on the report layout itself. She said, “The savings section catches attention immediately, which is good, but I wanted to understand why the recommendation was safe before fully trusting it.” She also mentioned, “The report looks professional, but some users may not understand whether these savings are guaranteed or estimated.” That made me realize the language around recommendations needed to feel more careful and realistic.

Another thing she pointed out was the balance between simplicity and explanation. She said, “Don’t make the report too long, because most people won’t read every section.” But later in the conversation she also said, “The recommendation cards should explain a little more about why a downgrade is okay.” That contradiction was actually helpful because it showed the report needed concise explanations rather than large blocks of text.

She also gave feedback about visual hierarchy. One thing she said was, “The total savings number should feel like the hero of the page, because that’s the first thing people care about.” Another comment was, “I would not share a report publicly if it showed company-specific details or internal spend information.”

The most surprising part of the conversation was that she trusted the report more after reading the reasoning sections, not from the large savings numbers themselves.

**Most surprising thing they said:** “The explanation matters more than the savings number. Big savings without reasoning feel less believable.”

**What changed afterward:** I improved the clarity of recommendation explanations, made the savings summary more visually prominent, adjusted report wording to sound less absolute, and ensured the public share flow excluded sensitive company information.
